Introduction¶

In this tutorial, I will walk you through the entire data science pipeline. To do this, I'll be making use of the tragedy of the Titanic, showing you how different factors had different effects on the survivability of individuals who were aboard the Titanic. I'll use data science to uncover the hidden patterns between who lived and who died on the night the Titanic sunk.

Why is that important? Understanding the patterns behind survival on the Titanic can help inform modern safety protocols in similar situations. By identifying the safety gaps that existed back then, we can uncover the systemic errors that led to such a large loss of life, and ensure that we never repeat them.

So, I now go forward with trying to answer the question: What factors affected survival the most on the Titanic?

Data Curation¶

I chose the Titanic - Machine Learning from Disaster dataset from Kaggle. This dataset has a wide variety of information on each passenger, and seemed fitting for further analysis.

By downloading the dataset, I obtained the following three files:

  • gender_submission.csv: Just a sample file showing how one is supposed to submit their responses to the Kaggle competition.
  • test.csv: Data to test on for the Kaggle competition, which is currently not useful to us because it doesn't have the true labels attached to it.
  • train.csv: Data to train our eventual model on.

To read the data, I just used the Pandas Python library. I also imported numpy and scipy to help with my analysis, and matplotlib and seaborn to help with my visualization. I also imported some helpful tools from the sklearn library.

The training data consists of the following features:

  1. PassengerId: A unique identifying number corresponding to each passenger
  2. Survived: Whether the passenger survived the tragedy (0 means no, 1 mean yes)
  3. Pclass: The class of the passenger's ticket (1st, 2nd, or 3rd)
  4. Name: Name of the passenger
  5. Sex: Sex of the passenger (Male or Female)
  6. Age: Age of the passenger in years
  7. SibSp: The number of siblings and spouses of the passenger aboard the Titanic
  8. Parch: The number of parents and children of the passenger aboard the Titanic
  9. Ticket: Ticket number of the passenger
  10. Fare: The passenger's fare
  11. Cabin: The cabin number of the passenger
  12. Embarked: The passenger's port of embarkation (Cherbourg, Queenstown, or Southampton)

Now, let's fill in the Age column by utilizing the median values across Sex and Pclass.

In [233]:
guess_ages = np.zeros((2, 3))

for i in range(2):
  for j in range(1, 4):
    guess_df = (train_df[(train_df['Sex'] == i) & (train_df['Pclass'] == j)]['Age']).dropna()
    age_guess = guess_df.median()
    guess_ages[i,j-1] = round(age_guess * 2) / 2

def replace_age(row):
  if pd.isna(row['Age']):
    return guess_ages[int(row['Sex']), int(row['Pclass']-1)]
  else:
    return row['Age']

train_df['Age'] = train_df.apply(replace_age, axis=1)

Finally, let's do a basic hypothesis test on the data.

H0: The class of a passenger's ticket DOES NOT have an effect on their likelihood of survival.

HA: The class of a passenger's ticket DOES have an effect on their likelihood of survival.

Assume alpha value of 0.05, and we'll apply a chi-square test because we want to see if there is a statistically significant relationship between the ticket class and the likelihood of the person surviving, which are two sets of categorical data.

Conclusions¶

My analysis revealed two features that impacted survivability on the Titanic the most: Sex and Ticket Class. Both of these appear to make sense. We know that women were given priority for lifeboats, which likely contributes to the strong correlation we see. Additionally, passengers in higher classes (lower pclass values) were located closest to the lifeboats, and thus could evacuate much quicker and easier. This raises questions about how people from different socioeconomic backgrounds are treated differently in emergency situations, suggesting that a higher socioeconomic status possibly affords one a better chance of survival.

One of the most surprising of my findings was that age appeared to not have much correlation with survival at all; before going into this project, I was under the impression that children were also given priority on lifeboats.

Ultimately, my insights align well with actual historical accounts of the Titanic, which emphasized the role of sex and ticket class in the survival of the passengers. My logistic regression was able to provide a decent understanding of the factors that went into survival on the Titanic.

However, this analysis is ultimately just a starting point. It is possible that with further research, more detailed passenger data, and more sophisticated analytical techniques, one could uncover even deeper insights into the factors that influenced survival aboard the Titanic.
